Source of Data

The data sets in this module were created using eChem, a package of R functions for simulating a variety of electrochemistry experiments. The simulations use the implicit finite difference method outlined in D. K. Gosser, Cyclic Voltammetry Simulation and Analysis of Reaction Mechanisms, VCH, New York, 1993 and in J.H. Brown “Development and Use of a Cyclic Voltammetry Simulator to Introduce Undergraduate Students to Electrochemical Simulations,” J. Chem. Educ., 2015, 92, 1490–1496 to calculate the current in chronoamperometry experiments; the charge in a chronocoulometry experiment is calculated using a trapezoidal integration of the corresponding chronoamperometry experiment. Although both of these resources were developed to simulate cyclic voltammetry experiments, their approach is easy to generalize to other diffusion-controlled electrochemistry experiments, such as chronoamperometry and chronocoulometry.

The eChem package is available from CRAN and from GitHub. In addition to a functions to simulate electrochemistry experiments, the eChem package includes functions to display the results of these experiments. One example is the plotGrid function, which is shown on the right. The central figure is the chronoamperogram for a chronoamperometry experiment. Surrounding the chronoamperogram are snapshots of the concentration profiles for Ox and Red at eight times—the time and the potential for each are listed above the chronoamperogram—during the experiment.

Additional Resources

For a general introduction to electrochemistry, see Chapter 11 (and, in particular, Chapter 11.4) of Analytical Chemistry 2.1. This resources is available through the LibreText project.

For more information about ladder diagrams, see Chapter 6.6 of Analytical Chemistry 2.1. This resources is available through the LibreText project.

Another useful general introduction of electrochemistry is Analytical Chemistry: The Basic Concepts. This resource is available through the Analytical Sciences Digital Library.

An excellent introduction to thinking about electrochemistry is the following paper: Larry R. Faulkner “Understanding electrochemistry: Some distinctive concepts”, J. Chem. Educ. 1983, 60, 262–264.

A description of the eChem package and its use in developing simulations is available in David T. Harvey, An-Phong Le, Charles A. Lucy, Brian M. Mosby, and Ellane J. Park, “The Use of Simulations with Active Learning Exercises,” Chapter 8 in Active Learning in the Analytical Chemistry Curriculum, ACS Symposium Series, Vol 1409, 2020, 121-145.
