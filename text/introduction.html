\(\require{mhchem} \ce{}\)In a chronoamperometry experiment we apply a potential pulse to an electrode and measure (‑ometry) the current (‑amper‑) that flows as a function of time (chrono‑) in an unstirred solution. The panel on the right provides four views of an experiment in which an oxidized species, Ox, undergoes an n electron reduction to give its reduced form, Red. \[\ce{Ox} + n e^{-} \ce{\rightleftharpoons} \ce{Red}\]

Ladder Diagram (upper left). A ladder (or predominance) diagram shows the relative stability of an analyte’s oxidized and reduced forms as a function of the applied potential and the analyte’s formal potential, which in this case is –\(0.25\) V. For potentials more positive than –\(0.25\) V, the analyte’s oxidized form is more stable than its reduced form, and for potentials more negative than –\(0.25\) V, the analyte’s reduced form is more stable than its oxidized form. At the formal potential, the oxidized and reduced forms are equally stable, and equal in concentration.

Potential Profile (upper right). In a chronoamperometry experiment, the applied potential profile has three parts: first, the potential is set and held constant at a value where the analyte does not undergo an oxidation or a reduction reaction; second, the potential is pulsed to a value where the analyte is oxidized or reduced; and, third, the applied potential is held at this value. In the experiment shown here a potential of 0.5 V is applied for \(10\) seconds, pulsed to a potential of –\(0.50\) V, and then held at this potential for an additional \(10\) seconds.

Diffusion Profiles (lower left). A diffusion profile shows the concentration of the analyte’s oxidized and reduced forms as a function of distance from the electrode’s surface at a specific time; here the diffusion profiles are shown at the beginning of the experiment (t = \(0\) s) and at the end of the experiment (t = \(20\) s).

Chronoamperogram (lower right). The chronoamperogram shows the current that flows as a function of time. This is the tangible result of a chronoamperometry experiment.
