The current in a chronoamperometry experiment is given by the Cottrell equation

\[i(t) = \frac{n F A C \sqrt{D}}{\sqrt{\pi}\sqrt{t}}\]

where \(i(t)\) is the current at a time, \(t\), after the pulse is applied, \(n\) is the number of electrons in the redox reaction, \(A\) is the area of the electrode in cm2, \(D\) is the diffusion coefficient for the electroactive species (Ox or Red) in cm2/s, \(C\) is its concentration in mol/L, and \(F\) is Faraday’s constant (96,458 C/mol, where C is a coulomb). You can adjust the values for \(n\), \(A\), \(D\), and \(C\) using the radio buttons above the chronoamperogram on the right. Values for the current, \(i(t)\), at integer times from 12 s to 20 s appear above the orange points that mark their corresponding times.

Exercise 1. Thinking about the experiment’s design (not the equation itself), explain why an increase in \(n\), \(A\), \(D\), and \(C\) results in a larger current, but an increase in \(t\) results in a smaller current.

Exercise 2. Use the radio buttons to verify that the relationship between \(i(t)\) and \(n\), \(A\), and \(C\) is linear, but that there is a square-root relationship between \(i(t)\) and \(D\), and an inverse square-root relationship between \(i(t)\) and \(t\).

Exercise 3. Current is measured in amperes (A), which is equivalent to coulombs per second; that is, 1 C/s = 1 A. Show that the units on the right side of the Cottrell equation yield a current in amperes; you do not need to use specific numerical values for any of the variables. Although we usually report concentration in mol/L, for this exercise you need to express volume using units of mol/cm3. Why?

Exercise 4. Set the radio buttons to \(n = 1\), \(A = 0.01 \text{ cm}^2\), \(D = 1\times10^{-5} \text{ cm}^2\text{/s}\), and \(C = 0.001 \text{ mol/L}\), and make note of the current in µA at a total elapsed time of 14 s. Verify that this current is correct by using the Cottrell equation to calculate the current. Be sure to consider your work on Exercise 3 and to consider the meaning of time in the Cottrell equation.
