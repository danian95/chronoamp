The four figures on the right describe a chronoamperometry experiment for the reversible one-electron reduction \(\ce{Ox} + e^- \ce{\rightleftharpoons} \ce{Red}\). The initial concentration of Ox is 1.0 mM and the initial concentration of Red is 0.0 mM. A potential of \(0.0 \text{ V}\) is maintained for 10 s and then pulsed to \(-0.5 \text{ V}\) where it is held for an additional 10 s. The solution remains unstirred throughout the experiment. You can use the slider to explore how the applied potential, the diffusion profiles for Ox and Red, and the current change with time during this 20 s experiment. Clicking on the slider’s play button steps through the experiment in 1 s intervals.

Exercise 1. Using the ladder diagram as a guide, will a redox reaction take place if the applied potential is pulsed from \(0 \text{ V}\) to \(-0.1 \text{ V}\)? What if the applied potential is pulsed from \(0 \text{ V}\) to \(-0.4 \text{ V}\)?

Exercise 2. Examine the diffusion profiles for Ox and for Red at times \(t = 0\), \(t = 5\), \(t = 10\), \(t = 15\), and \(t = 20\) s, and describe features they share and features they do not share.

Exercise 3. Examine the diffusion profiles for Ox and for Red at \(t = 20\) and note that the concentration of Ox is 0 mM at the electrode’s surface, but 1.0 mM when sufficiently far from the electrode. Why is there no Ox at the electrode’s surface? Explain why the concentration of Ox is not 0 mM at all distances from the electrode’s surface. It might be useful to know that the electrode has a surface area of 0.01 cm2 and that the sample has a volume on the order of 50 mL.

Exercise 4. Current is a measure of the rate at which Ox is reduced to Red—or the rate at which Red is oxidized to Ox—and is proportional to the slope of the diffusion profile evaluated at the electrode’s surface. Given this, explain (a) why the current is 0 when \(t < 10 \text{ s}\); (b) why the current spikes to its maximum value when \(t = 10 \text{ s}\); and (c) why the current decreases when \(t > 10 \text{ s}\).
