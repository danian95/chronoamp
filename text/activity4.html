Integrating the current in a chronoamperometry experiment as a function of time gives the cumulative charge, \(Q\), in coulombs passed during the redox reaction. In a chronocoulometry experiment we take the output of a chronoamperometry experiment and run it through an integrator. The result is a plot of \(Q\) vs. \(t\).

Exercise 1. Examine the chronoamperometry experiment in the module’s tab for a typical experiment and review what you learned about the shape of the current trace as a function of time. Think about what it means to integrate a function and predict the shape of the corresponding plot of \(Q\) vs. \(t\). Use the slider to test your prediction and comment on your results. Clicking on the slider’s play button steps through the experiment in 1 s intervals.

Exercise 2. What advantage(s) might chronocoulometry have over chronoamperometry? Consider, for example, using chronoamperometry and chronocoulometry to determine the concentration of an analyte in a sample. Which method would you use and why?
